Genome sequencing, end to end

A technical primer on how a tube of saliva becomes a list of variants — the biology, the instruments, the data formats and algorithms, and the hard limits on what any of it means. Every non-obvious claim is cited; sources are listed at the bottom.

1What is actually being sequenced

Your genome is ~3.1 billion base pairs of DNA, spooled across 23 chromosome pairs plus a tiny circular mitochondrial genome (16,569 bp). The 2022 telomere-to-telomere assembly (T2T-CHM13) — the first truly gapless human genome — measured 3,054,815,472 nuclear base pairs.1 Of that, only ~20,000 protein-coding genes (about 1–2% of the sequence) code for proteins; the exact count is still argued over.2

DNA is a four-letter code — A C G T (adenine, cytosine, guanine, thymine). It's double-stranded and complementary (A pairs with T, C with G), which is what makes it copyable and readable. You inherit one copy of each chromosome from each parent, so at any position you have two alleles (your genotype): homozygous if they match, heterozygous if they differ.

How your genome differs from "the reference"

There is no single normal genome. Analysis is done by comparison to a reference assembly, and a typical person differs from it at 4–5 million sites, >99.9% of which are single-nucleotide variants and short insertions/deletions.3 Variant classes, smallest to largest:

  • SNV (single-nucleotide variant) — one base changed, e.g. A→G. The vast majority of your variants.
  • Indel — a short insertion or deletion (1–50 bp). Harder to call than SNVs.
  • SV / CNV — structural variants and copy-number variants (>50 bp: large deletions, duplications, inversions, translocations). Clinically important, and the hardest to detect with short reads.

Genomic coordinates (a position like chr7:117,559,590) are only meaningful relative to a build. Mixing GRCh37 and GRCh38 data silently mis-places everything — one of the most common and damaging mistakes in a home pipeline.8

2How sequencing actually works

No instrument reads a chromosome from one end to the other. Instead, sequencing is shotgun: shatter the genome into millions of small fragments, read the fragments, and reassemble by mapping them back to the reference. Platforms differ mainly in read length and error profile.

Coverage (depth), and why 30× is the magic number

Coverage is the average number of independent reads overlapping each position. Because fragments land roughly at random, the number of reads at any position follows a Poisson distribution (the Lander–Waterman model).7a At 30×, essentially every position is covered enough times that you can distinguish a true heterozygous site (~half the reads carry the variant) from sequencing noise, and confidently call homozygous vs heterozygous genotypes — which is why 30× (≈90 gigabases of data per genome) is the germline clinical-grade standard.7b Below ~15× you start missing heterozygotes; "low-pass" WGS (~0.4×) is far cheaper but only works with statistical imputation.

The cost curve

Sequencing has fallen faster than Moore's law. The symbolic "$1000 genome" arrived in 2014; the last figure in the NHGRI cost-tracking series was ~$525 per genome (2022), and newer instruments now advertise sub-$200 — though those are vendor list prices, not independent benchmarks.9

3From reads to variants — the data & the pipeline

The instrument's raw output is not a genome; it's a giant pile of short reads. Three file formats carry the data through the pipeline, each a published standard maintained by the community:

FormatHoldsStage
FASTQRaw reads + a per-base quality scoreOff the sequencer12a
BAM / CRAMReads aligned to reference coordinatesAfter alignment12b
VCFThe variants — where you differ from the referenceAfter variant calling12c

Quality scores (Phred) — the number everything hinges on

Each base in a FASTQ read carries a Phred quality score Q = −10·log₁₀(Perror). So Q30 = a 1-in-1,000 chance the base is wrong (99.9% accurate), Q20 = 1-in-100. Every downstream step — alignment confidence, variant-call confidence — is a probabilistic combination of these per-base error estimates.

The stages (this is exactly what the app runs)

FASTQ ──align (bwa-mem2)──▶ BAM ──mark dups + recalibrate──▶ analysis-ready BAM ──call (GATK / DeepVariant / Parabricks)──▶ VCF VCF ──annotate (VEP × ClinVar)──▶ health · ──project vs 1000G──▶ ancestry
  • Align. Each read is placed at its best-matching position in the reference (BWA-MEM / bwa-mem2). This is the hard, compute-heavy step — millions of reads, each searched against 3 billion bases.
  • Refine. Mark PCR duplicates (artificial copies from library prep that would inflate confidence) and, in the GATK path, recalibrate base-quality scores (BQSR) against known-variant sites.
  • Call variants. Two dominant approaches: GATK HaplotypeCaller locally re-assembles reads and evaluates candidate haplotypes with a statistical model; DeepVariant reformulates calling as image classification and runs a convolutional neural net over pileup images.7c NVIDIA Parabricks (this app's GPU path) is a hardware-accelerated re-implementation of the same alignment + calling.
  • Annotate. Tools like VEP attach meaning to each variant: which gene, predicted effect, and cross-references to clinical databases (ClinVar).

How do you know the calls are right? Benchmarking

You can't eyeball 4 million variants. Instead, the field uses truth sets: NIST's Genome in a Bottle (GIAB) project has exhaustively characterized a handful of genomes (e.g. HG002) to produce high-confidence "answer keys," and GA4GH standardizes the comparison tools (hap.py, vcfeval) that report precision, recall, and F1.7d On these benchmarks, both GATK HaplotypeCaller and DeepVariant exceed F1 ≈ 0.99 for SNVs — the top precisionFDA Truth Challenge entry reached ~0.9996.7e (This app hit precision 99.2% / recall 99.5% on GIAB HG002 chr20 — right in that band.)

Clinical significance is a spectrum, not a verdict

The ACMG/AMP framework sorts variants into five tiers: Pathogenic, Likely Pathogenic, Uncertain Significance (VUS), Likely Benign, Benign.10a Most of what a raw genome surfaces is VUS — genuinely unknown. And even a real Pathogenic call is not a diagnosis:

  • Penetrance — not everyone carrying a pathogenic variant develops the condition. A BRCA1 variant raises cancer risk substantially but is not a certainty.10c
  • Expressivity — among those affected, severity and features vary widely.
  • Zygosity & inheritance — one pathogenic copy of a recessive gene makes you a healthy carrier (dormant); a dominant gene needs only one. This is exactly the split the app's "hereditary conditions" report computes.

For deliberate screening, the ACMG publishes a short list of medically actionable "secondary findings" genes worth reporting — 84 genes in the current SF v3.3 (2025).10b

◆
Why raw genomes over-alarm. A healthy genome carries a handful of truly pathogenic variants among tens of thousands of benign/uncertain ClinVar hits, plus false positives in hard-to-sequence regions and coverage gaps. Filtering, zygosity, penetrance and confirmatory testing are what turn a scary list into a real signal — which is why every health output here says research-grade, see a genetic counselor.

Ancestry is directional, not destiny

Ancestry works by projecting your genotypes onto a reference panel (this app uses PCA against the 1000 Genomes superpopulations). With a public panel of a few thousand people it recovers broad continental structure well, but it cannot match the resolution of a proprietary database of millions — treat proportions as similarity to reference populations, not literal descent.

5Ethics, privacy & law

Your genome is the one identifier you can never change, it implicates blood relatives who never consented, and it is legible to insurers and databases decades from now. A few things to understand before you sequence:

  • GINA has a hole in it. The US Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (2008) bars genetic discrimination in health insurance and employment — but explicitly not in life, disability, or long-term-care insurance, where insurers may use genetic and family-history information (a few states add protections).11
  • Data permanence & jurisdiction. Once a vendor holds your sequence it can be copied, cached, or subpoenaed. Prefer providers that let you fully download and delete your data, and note where the company and lab are legally based.
  • Incidental findings. Decide before you look whether you want to know about unexpected serious findings (e.g. a cancer-risk variant you weren't screening for). This is a genuine and irreversible choice.
  • Relatives. Your data reveals probabilistic information about siblings, parents, and children — sequencing yourself is partly a decision on their behalf.

Running the pipeline yourself, locally, is one of the strongest privacy postures available: the raw data and the interpretation never leave your own machine.
